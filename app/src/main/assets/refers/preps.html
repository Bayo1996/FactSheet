RAFIKI

PrEP = Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis

ART taken on a daily basis by HIV negative people who are at high risk to acquire HIV infection

The goal is to prevent HIV infection

Who should consider taking PrEP?

  1. Sexual partner is HIV positive and one of the following:
    • Not on ART
    • On ART < 6 months
    • Suspected poor medication adherence
    • Most recent viral load is detectable
  2. Sexual partner/s have an unknown HIV status and are at high risk of infection
    • Multiple sexual partners
    • History of sexually transmitted infections (STIs)
    • Engages in transactional sex
    • Injects drugs
    • From a high HIV positive area
    • Engage in transactional sex
    • Recurrent use of PEP
    • History of sex while under the influence of alcohol or drugs
    • Inconsistent or no condom use with a partner that is HIV positive or their status is unknown
    • Sero-discordant couples trying to get pregnant

What can PrEP do?

Studies show it can reduce the risk of HIV infection by up to 90%

What does PrEP NOT do?

PrEP DOES NOT:

  • Guarantee 100% protection from HIV infection
  • Prevent other sexually transmitted infections (STIs)
  • Prevent pregnancy
  • Cure HIV
  • Work as a treatment for HIV

IMPLEMENTING PrEP

Risk Assessment
  • You must determine the patient’s risk for acquiring HIV
  • Provide a private space in which to talk
  • Establish rapport with the client
  • Assure confidentiality
  • Use a non-judgmental attitude
  • Allow the client to ask questions
In the last 6 months:
  • Have you been sexually active?
  • Have you had more than one sexual partner?
  • Did you have sexual contact with a partner without using a condom?
  • How many of your sexual partners are HIV positive or have unknown status?
  • Have you had sex with a HIV-positive/status unknown partner without a condom?
  • Have you been treated for a sexually transmitted infection?
  • Have you injected drugs (not from your doctor)?
  • If you injected drugs, did you use a needle or syringe that another person had used?
  • Have you had sex while you or your partner was under the influence of alcohol or drugs?
  • Are you in a HIV-discordant couple? Is it a new diagnosis? What is the positive partner’s viral load?

Eligibility requirements for PrEP

  • Person is confirmed HIV negative
  • ≥ 15 years old and ≥ 35 kg

  • Has no current or recent illness (past 1 month) consistent with acute HIV infection…

    Fever, sore throat, swollen glands, diarrhea, headache, muscle or joint pains

    …and a preceding high risk HIV exposure

  • Normal kidney function
  • No contraindication to TDF ± FTC or 3TC
  • Ready to commit to the medication
  • Willing to attend follow-up appointments
  • Willing to get repeat HIV testing

Laboratory Requirements

  • Rapid HIV test that is negative from that day
  • If possible:
    • Creatinine (especially if risk factors like age>65, diabetes, uncontrolled hypertension, HBV/HCV)
    • HepB surface antigen
    • HCV serology

When to prescribe PrEP?

  • Patient proven to be HIV negative
  • Patient has risk factor for becoming HIV positive
  • Patient has completed necessary lab work
  • Patient has been screened for STIs and pregnancy
  • Patient understands risks and benefits of PrEP
  • Patient is ready and willing to start PrEP

Initial Prescription

TDF + FTC = Truvada

Alternate regimen if needed

1 pill once a day

Give a one month supply

Offer condoms

Schedule return visit in one month

Follow-up

Patient RTC at xone month and then every 3 months

Assess for side effects; creatinine check at 12 mos.

Patient must be HIV tested and remain negative

Counseling on risk reduction

Assess for PrEP continuation vs discontinuation

Counseling on adherence, adherence, adherence!

Other Considerations

If HIV test becomes positive, STOP PrEP!

Treat symptoms of STIs

Manage side effects

If concern for acute HIV infection while on PrEP:

  • Do HIV rapid test
  • Repeat HIV rapid test in one month

PrEP is okay in women who are pregnant or breastfeeding

When to stop PrEP

  • HIV positive
  • Change in risk status (to low risk)
  • Continue 28 days after risk status changes
  • Renal dysfunction (CrCl 50L/min )
  • Client asks to stop
  • Sustained non-adherence
  • Sustained viral suppression in HIV+ partner (continue to promote condom use)

Challenges to providing PrEP

  • Unwillingness to know HIV status
  • Stigma
  • Return appointments
  • Adherence
