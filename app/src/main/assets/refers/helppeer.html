Pressures

Transition into college and becoming a teenager/youth can be very challenging for children. Some changes include added pressures from friends and peers.

Pressures are a normal part of life and young people need guidance from their lectures, parents and other adults so that they are able to handle these pressures in a positive way.

Some of these pressures may be drugs, truancy, sex, shop-lifting, bullying, cheating, and any other action that a child may not want to do.

What you can do?

Make students aware of some of the pressures they may encounter

Demonstrate the difference between positive and negative peer pressure

Provide suggestions and strategies to help children deal with peer pressure

Positive vs. Negative Peer Pressure

Negative Peer Pressure-

Is often dangerous and against school rules, home rules and personal values.

  • Skipping school
  • Vandalizing
  • Smoking
  • Sneaking out of the house
  • Bullying
  • Disrespecting authority
  • Sex

Positive Peer Pressure-

Is often overlooked but does existand may be described as an influence to do what is right.

  • Studying
  • Volunteering
  • Befriending someone
  • Community Service
  • Joining a sports team

Identifying Students

Traits putting students at a higher risk of falling to peer pressure

  • Low self esteem
  • Lack of confidence
  • Uncertainty about ones place within a given peer group
  • No personal interests exclusive of one's peer group
  • Feeling isolated from peers and/or family
  • Lack of direction in life
  • Depression
  • Eating disorders
  • Poor academic abilities or performance

Steps children can follow when confronted with peer pressure:

  1. Ask Questions

    2. “Why would we do that ?”, “Whose idea was this ?”, “Is this a smart thing to do ?”

  2. Identify the negative behavior or action

    3. “Calling her names is just going to start trouble”, “ don’t think smoking is a good idea”, “It is against school policy to leave the grounds”.

  3. Evaluate the consequences

    4. “We will get in trouble”, “Smoking is not healthy”, “My parents will take away my allowance”

  4. Suggest an alternative

    5. “Why don’t we go to the store after school is over”

  5. Leave the situation

    6. If all else fails, remove yourself from the situation. Walk away and do something else

Positive and Healthy Ways to Deal with Pressures

Strategies for students to use:

  1. Make a joke and change the subject
  2. Say “no” and keep saying “no”
  3. Leave the area
  4. Get help from someone you trust
  5. Suggest a different activity
  6. Hang out with others who share your beliefs

Help students develop decision making skills

Nurture teenagers' abilities and self-esteem so they can forge positive peer relationships

Empower parents and educators to help teenagers pursue and maintain positive peer relationships

Encourage cross-ethnic and "cross-class" peer interactions and guide teenagers in dealing positively with cultural diversity and individual differences.

Support parent education programs for families with teenagers

Establish intervention programs for preadolescents with low social skills or aggressive tendencies.
