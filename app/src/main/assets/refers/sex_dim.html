Human Sexuality Human sexuality has at least four dimensions – biological, psychological, ethical and cultural. The interrelationship of these dimensions results in an individual’s total sexuality.

Biological Dimensions

The biological dimension of sexuality involves: our physical appearance, especially the development of physical sexual characteristics; our responses to sexual stimulation; our ability to reproduce or to control fertility and development in general.

  • Although human reproductive functioning doesn’t begin until puberty, human sexual-erotic functioning begins immediately after birth and lasts a lifetime. It is important to realize that biological functioning, as it related to sexuality, is a part of the natural functioning of humans.

    • Psychological Dimension

    The psychological dimension of sexuality embraces our attitudes toward ourselves and other people, attitudes that accumulate from experience.

  • From birth onwards, we receive signals from all around us telling us how to think or act. We learn how people of our sex ‘should’ behave, that some words are ‘wrong’ or ‘dirty’, that certain parts of our bodies are untouchable and unmentionable (at least in certain circumstances), and that we should hide our feelings if we think they are unacceptable to others. Some of us are lucky enough to grow up with a more set of experiences, but regardless of whether our experiences are positive or negative, our learned responses to them become integral to our sexuality.

    • Ethical Dimension

    The ethical dimension of sexuality involves questions about the way we treat ourselves and other people: “Should I or shouldn’t I participate in a certain sexual behavior?”

    How we deliberate on such questions and ultimately decide what is right and wrong profoundly shapes our sexuality, whether the approach to deciding is religious, humanistic, or pragmatic.

    Cultural Dimension

    The cultural dimension of sexuality is the sum of the cultural influences that affect our thoughts and actions, both historical and contemporary. Historical influences become evident when one considers roles of males and females, as well as certain customs. Among the sources of current influences is radio, television, film, inter-personal relationships. Each impresses upon us the culturally defined ways in which we “ought” to think as females and males and the roles our sexuality “ought” to lead us and to play.
