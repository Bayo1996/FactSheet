Counselling

Is a person-to-person interaction, in which the peer provides adequate information to enable the client to understand his or her problem better in order to make an informed decision and act upon it.

Value

Is a belief that is important to an individual. Values can be influenced by religious, educational or cultural factors, as well as personal experiences.

Attitude

Is a view or opinion that is formed by values and beliefs (self esteem, hope, faith, confidence).

Interpersonal relationship

Is the ability to get along with other people in order to achieve common goals.

The principle of interpersonal relationship is: “seek first to understand, then be understood.” Deeply understand the problem. Build the skills of empathetic listening on the basis of openness and trust.

There is an overlap between the two terms (values and attitudes) as someone’s personal attitudes or opinions may be based on the values that he or she holds.

The way we express our attitudes, feelings, biases and values will affect how well we counsel our clients. For example, if we are judgmental in our private reaction to the way the client looks, his or her social class, religion or cultural factors, counselling will not be effective

Qualities of a good peer counselor

Peer providers who counsel young people must possess certain personal qualities, attitudes, knowledge and skills. The counselor should posses the following qualities:

  • Enthusiasm to work with and help young people
  • Belief in the value of providing ASRH services
  • Respect young people and their rights to make decisions for themselves
  • Should be comfortable with expression of feelings
  • Aware of one’s values and limitations

Counseling skills and techniques

There are many counseling skills that peer counselors need to apply during their practice to enable them be effective in helping their clients make informed choices.

Note that young people are very sensitive

  • Asking questions
  • Showing empathy
  • Clarifying, paraphrasing and reflecting

Counseling skills and techniques

Active Listening

  • Listening is not the same as hearing. It is not a natural process, but rather requires energy, skill and commitment.
  • Although listening is considered in many cultures to be passive and less important than speaking, active listening is a powerful tool because it can make the client feel important, acknowledged, and empowered.

Active listening can be improved by:

  • Maintaining eye contact
  • Demonstrating interest
  • Being attentive to the young person (e.g., not doing other tasks at the same time and not interrupting)

Effective questioning skills

  • Open and closed ended questions are used to identify, clarify and break down the problem into component parts.
  • Closed-ended questions may be suitable to determine the young person’s condition and medical history at the beginning of counseling.
  • Closed-ended questions can be answered by yes, no, a number or a few words. They can be used at the beginning of counselling to break the ice, since they do not require the young person to reveal sensitive information or to share feelings. An example of a closed question is, "How many girlfriends do you have?”
  • Open-ended questions are useful for exploring the opinions and feeling of young people and usually require longer responses. These questions are more effective in determining their needs in terms of information and what they already know.
  • Open-ended questions have many possible Answers. They can be used to learn about the client’s feelings, thoughts, knowledge and beliefs. An example is, "How does your partner feel about condoms?” Asking question that begin with “why” may be intimidating or seem judgmental. It is preferable to use phrases such as, “What are your reasons for…?”
  • Probing questions helps the counselor to clarify the young person’s responses to open questions. An example is, “Can you tell me how your friend’s experience has made you feel about abortion?”
  • Leading questionslead the respondent in a certain direction and are not desirable in counselling situations.“Isn’t it normal for boys to have wet dreams?”

Attending skills

SOLER is an acronym used to describe the attending skills for the counsellor. Attending skills communicates to the clients that you are there for them.

S – Sit squarely
O – Open posture
L – Lean forward
E – Eye contact
R – Relax

Reflection:

Reflection is the echoing back of the last few words that the client has spoken.

Challenging:

Though challenging and confronting are not associated with counselling, there are times they are appropriate and even necessary.

Paraphrasing

The counsellor rephrases the content of the client’s message

Purposes of Paraphrasing

  • To convey that you are understanding him/her
  • Help the client by simplifying, focusing and crystallizing what they said May encourage the client to elaborate
  • Provide a check on the accuracy of your perceptions

ROLES OF A PEER EDUCATOR

DEFINATION OF A PEER EDUCATOR

Peer educators are trained people who assist others in peer group [a group of people like themselves] to make informed decisions about life (social, sexuality, health, development).

Peer education focuses on behavior and social change.

Examples of Peer Educators include:

 
  • A youth footballer who is discussing about substance abuse with fellow players.
  • A commercial sex worker is discussing treatment for STIs with other sex workers.
  • A woman is discussing about condoms with other women in her merry- go -round group, gives condom demonstration and has some for distribution.
  • A student who approaches her/his colleague to discuss about boy/girl relationships.
  • A young person who talks to a fellow youth about his/her fears about STIs.

Tasks/Roles of a peer educator as follows:

  • Hold one to one sessions with peers to discuss problems or life’s issues that they bring up
  • Refers peers to appropriate services e.g. drug abuse rehabilitation centers, VCT, Antenatal Care, contraception.
  • If sexually active youth, they may help access condoms.
  • Mobilize community groups for various activities, or actions e.g. World AIDS Day, Education Day, World Population Day, Environment Day etc.
  • Recruit/ Mobilize peers to discuss on various issues.
  • Organize activities (football, netball, drama etc.) to occupy the adolescence
  • Keep records on peer education activities.
  • Participate in supervision, monitoring and evaluation

Qualities of good peer educators

  • Popular and influential among peers
  • Concerned about the welfare of others
  • Able to listen actively
  • Self confident
  • Dependable and honest
  • Team-builder and leader.
  • Assertive

Some challenges faced by peer educators:

  • Peer educators require extra time and commitment.
  • Maintaining motivation
  • Jealousy among peers
  • Some peer educators are engaging in risky behaviors
  • Lack of adequate knowledge and skills

Principle behind experiential learning:

  • What I hear, I forget,
  • What I hear and see, I remember a little.
  • What I hear I see and ask questions about or discuss with someone else, I begin to understand.
  • What I hear, see, discuss, and do allows me to acquire knowledge and skill.
  • What I teach to another, I master.
